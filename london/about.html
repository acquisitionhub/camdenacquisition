I Left Paris to Build a Business
in London With No Network,
No Clients, and No Safety Net.

Julien Kabongo · Founder, Camden Acquisition Hub · Originally published April 2026

I help Camden dental practices recover missed-call revenue automatically — no extra staff, no new workflows. Here's how I got here.

When people find out I moved from Paris to build a business in the UK, they usually say one of two things.

Either: "Oh, London is amazing — you must love it."

Or: "That's brave."

The second one is closer to the truth. But brave isn't quite the right word either. Slightly naive, maybe. Stubbornly optimistic, definitely.

I first came to London in 2016 — not for a business idea, but for something simpler and more personal. I wanted a better quality of life. I wanted to be genuinely fluent and bilingual, not just conversational. I wanted to immerse myself fully in British culture, not observe it from a distance. So I came, and I stayed. And over the years, London stopped feeling like a place I'd moved to and started feeling like home.

"I didn't come to London with a client list. I didn't come with investors, or a mentor, or even a friend who could introduce me to the right people. I came with a frustration I couldn't shake — and an idea I thought was worth building."

The frustration that started everything

Dental marketing in the UK is, for the most part, genuinely bad. Not bad in a subtle way. Bad in the way where practices are paying agencies thousands of pounds a month for generic strategies that could apply to a car dealership or a restaurant. Bad in the way where a dentist in Camden is being marketed to people in Brighton. Bad in the way where nobody seems to be asking the most basic question: where are the actual patients going, and why aren't they booking here?

I'd been working in marketing long enough to see the pattern. And the more I looked at the dental sector specifically, the more I saw the same gap everywhere: practices losing real revenue from missed calls — a patient rings, nobody picks up, and within minutes that person has booked somewhere else. Not because they preferred the competitor. Just because the competitor answered.

It felt like an obvious problem. I couldn't understand why nobody was solving it properly. So I decided to try.

What nobody tells you about building as an outsider

The professional isolation is real. When everyone around you talks about "warm outreach" and "leveraging your network," there's a quiet moment where you realise — I don't have that here. My network is in Paris. My contacts speak French. My context is different.

For a while, I let that feel like a disadvantage.

Then I started to notice something. Because I was an outsider, I was asking questions that people embedded in the industry had stopped asking. I wasn't assuming anything about how dental marketing "worked" in the UK. I was looking at it fresh — and what I saw was a lot of expensive activity producing very little in the way of actual booked patients.

"The outsider perspective, it turns out, isn't a handicap. It's a lens."

Where I am now

I'm still early in this journey. Camden Acquisition Hub is young. I'm building it deliberately — one practice at a time, one postcode at a time, starting with Camden where I know the competitive landscape intimately.

I'm not going to pretend I have a decade of results to show you. What I have is a clear focus, a system I believe in, and a genuine obsession with one problem: helping dental practices in NW1 stop losing patients they don't even know they're losing.

I'm also building something I'm proud of — not just as a business, but as proof that you can arrive somewhere new with nothing but an idea and a work ethic, and make something real out of it.

Let's connect

If you're a dental practice owner in Camden or North London, I'd genuinely love to connect — not to pitch you, but because understanding your day-to-day is how I make what I'm building actually useful.

And if you're someone navigating a similar path — building something in a country that isn't yours, figuring it out without a ready-made network — I'd love to hear your story too. That's the kind of connection that actually means something.
