How London Dentists Get More Google Reviews (And Why It Changes Everything)

A practical 2026 guide to collecting more 5-star reviews, climbing the Maps pack, and turning your online reputation into your best patient acquisition tool.

By the Patient Acquisition Hub team · Updated April 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to Patient Acquisition Hub

If you run a dental practice in London, you already know the city is competitive. There are over 3,000 practices across Greater London — and they're all fighting for the same three spots in the Google Maps pack.

The practices that win those spots aren't necessarily the best dentists. They're the ones with the most recent, high-quality Google reviews. In this guide, we'll break down exactly why reviews matter so much, how the top-ranking London practices collect them, and what you can do to catch up — or pull further ahead.

Why Google Reviews Are the Most Important Marketing Tool for London Dentists

Before a patient calls your practice, they almost certainly look you up on Google. Research consistently shows that around 84% of people read online reviews before making a healthcare decision — and dental practices are no exception.

But it's not just about persuading individual patients. Google uses review signals as a direct ranking factor for local search results. When someone searches "dentist near me" or "private dentist Islington," Google's algorithm looks at your review count, your average rating, and how recently reviews were posted — and ranks you accordingly.

The London reality: In smaller UK cities, 50–80 reviews might be enough to put you in the top 3 on Google Maps. In central London, top-ranking practices often have 200–400+ reviews with a 4.8★ average. The bar is higher — and it keeps rising.

The 5 Things That Determine Your Google Maps Ranking

Google's local ranking algorithm is complex, but for dental practices in London, these five factors matter most:

1. Review Volume

The sheer number of reviews you have is one of the strongest signals Google uses. A practice with 300 reviews will almost always outrank one with 40, all else being equal. The gap between practices in London is significant — many have fewer than 50 reviews while their competitors sit at 200+.

2. Review Recency

A burst of 100 reviews from two years ago is worth less than 20 reviews from the last three months. Google prioritises freshness — it wants to know that your practice is still active and still delivering good experiences. This is why you need a consistent, ongoing review collection strategy, not a one-time push.

3. Average Star Rating

Your star rating affects both your ranking and your click-through rate. Patients are unlikely to even click on a practice with fewer than 4 stars — and Google knows this. Aim for 4.7★ or above. If you're below that, focus first on generating more positive reviews to dilute any older negative ones.

4. Response Rate

Responding to your Google reviews — both positive and negative — signals to Google that your business is active and engaged. Practices that respond to reviews consistently tend to rank higher than those that don't. It also shows prospective patients that you care.

5. Google Business Profile Completeness

Make sure your profile has up-to-date opening hours, photos, services, and a description. An incomplete profile is a missed opportunity — and Google factors this into local rankings.

Why Most Dental Practices Struggle to Collect Reviews

Every dentist we speak to tells us the same thing: "We know we should ask for reviews — we just never do it consistently."

And that's completely understandable. Receptionists are busy. Dentists are focused on patients. By the time a patient leaves, the team has moved on to the next appointment. Asking for a review feels awkward, and even when you do ask, most patients forget by the time they get home.

This is why the practices that dominate London's Google Maps results don't rely on manual asking. They use automated systems that send a personalised review request the moment a patient leaves their appointment — while the experience is fresh and the patient is still on their phone.

The Right Way to Collect Google Reviews (Without Being Pushy)

Timing is everything

The best time to ask for a review is within 30–60 minutes of the appointment ending. At that point, the patient has just had a positive experience, they're back on their phone, and it takes about 60 seconds to leave a review. Wait until the next day and the window is largely gone.

Make it one tap

Your review request should include a direct link to your Google Business Profile review page. No searching for your practice, no signing into accounts, no hunting for where to click. One tap and they're there. This alone dramatically increases the response rate.

Use SMS, not email

Email open rates for dental practices tend to hover around 20–30%. SMS open rates are consistently above 90%, and most people read texts within minutes. For review requests, SMS wins every time.

Route unhappy patients privately

Not every appointment ends perfectly. Instead of hoping unhappy patients don't post a negative review, a smart system routes them to a private feedback form first — giving you the chance to resolve the issue before it goes public. This protects your rating without suppressing legitimate feedback.

What Results Can You Realistically Expect?

London practices using an automated review collection system typically see a 4–8× increase in monthly review volume within 90 days. For a practice currently getting 2–3 reviews a month, that's 10–20 new reviews every month — adding up to 120–240 new reviews in a year.

Google Maps ranking improvements typically become visible within 4–8 weeks as your review velocity improves. The practices that commit to this consistently are the ones that dominate local search results 12–18 months later.

Getting Started: Your Next Steps

If you're serious about improving your Google Maps ranking in London, here's what to focus on first:

  • Claim and complete your Google Business Profile — make sure hours, services, and photos are up to date.
  • Set up an automated review request system — manual asking simply doesn't scale.
  • Start responding to your existing reviews — both positive and negative. Even a simple "Thank you, we're so glad you had a great experience!" makes a difference.
  • Track your review velocity — how many new reviews you're getting per month. That number should be going up consistently.
